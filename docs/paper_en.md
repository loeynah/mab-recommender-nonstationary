Multi-Armed Bandit Algorithms for Recommender Systems in Non-Stationary Environments

Abstract. Multi-armed bandit (MAB) algorithms have been widely applied in recommendation systems. However, when the system operates in a non-stationary environment, traditional MAB methods encounter fundamental challenges: they often presuppose a static arm set and stationary reward distributions, overvalue stale observations after distribution shifts, and adjust slowly to newly appearing or disappearing items. Moreover, many variants demand a specified horizon or are vulnerable to exploration constants, becoming fragile under temporal drift. They also lack principled solutions to cold-start items and popularity fluctuations, resulting in poor recommendations in practice. To overcome these challenges, we present three improved variants: the dynamic arm management UCB algorithm to efficiently manage time-varying recommendable item sets; the popularity-aware UCB algorithm to utilize item popularity information for better decision accuracy; and the cold-start UCB algorithm to alleviate the cold-start issue for new items via knowledge transfer using a similarity matrix. Experiments on the MovieLens 25M dataset show that the proposed methods obtain much higher cumulative rewards than the standard UCB algorithm, and perform better in dealing with dynamic arm sets and cold-start situations. Together, these methods provide a complete and complementary approach to improve recommendation systems in non-stationary environments.
Keywords: Multi-armed bandit, Bandit algorithms, Recommender systems, Non-stationary environment, Dynamic arm management.
Introduction
Recommender systems are crucial for information retrieval and personalization. The growth of the internet has resulted in increasing amounts of data, where both user preferences and item popularity may change over time. This non-stationarity causes sparse or delayed feedback and poses significant challenges to the design and implementation of recommender systems. Thus, it is necessary that algorithms be able to flexibly adapt to these changes.
MAB algorithms have received extensive interests as an online learning framework for the explorationexploitation dilemma in recommendation. The essence is to make better decisions via repeated trials and feedback to accumulate the maximum rewards. Nevertheless, classical MAB approaches perform poorly in non-stationary scenarios. Firstly, the arm set is static and does not incorporate new items or retire stale items with the passage of time. Secondly, the popularity of items and the interest of users vary over time, and classical approaches neglect the temporal evolution and might recommend items with inferior rewards according to historical data. Thirdly, new users and items do not have historical information, and it is challenging for existing algorithms to cope with cold starts and evaluate the value of them. All these challenges call for MAB algorithms that can adapt to a dynamic arm set and exploit knowledge transfer to mitigate cold starts [1][2].
To overcome these challenges, we design and implement three strategies for non-stationary recommendation: Dynamic-Arm-Management UCB, Popularity-Aware UCB, and Cold-Start UCB. We evaluate the methods on the large scale MovieLens 25M dataset for performance in dynamic environments. The innovations are a dynamic arm management mechanism, the incorporation of temporal popularity information, and knowledge transfer through similarity matrices. By combining system dynamics with user feedback, the design closes the loop to achieve a collaborative effect, ease the explorationexploitation dilemma, and offer a new solution to deployment in non-stationary scenarios.
Traditional Strategies and their Limitations
The MAB problem is fundamental in reinforcement learning, seeking to maximize cumulative reward under limited exploration. Classical algorithms generally assume stationarity, i.e., arm reward distributions are fixed during learning. Representative methods include ε-greedy, UCB, and Thompson Sampling, but they lose effectiveness under non-stationarity.
ε-greedy balances exploration and exploitation by selecting the empirically best arm with probability 1 − ε and a random arm with probability ε at time t. Despite its simplicity, it has fundamental disadvantages: for constant ε the regret is linear O\left(\varepsilon T\right) and does not converge; for decaying ε, the schedule needs to be carefully crafted, and random exploration is wasteful. Following shifts, the fixed exploration probability hinders quick identification of the new best arm resulting in linerly growing regret.
Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) chooses the arm with the highest upper confidence bound, balancing empirical mean and uncertainty. Its regret upper bound in the stationarity is O\left(\sqrt{\left(KTlogT\right)}\right) and performance is good there [3]. However, standard UCB typically needs the horizon T, over-weights old data after changes, and is sensitive to constants, which makes it difficult to turn. 
Thompson Sampling samples from posterior beliefs to decide which arm to pull. Despite good theoretical guarantees, it may incur high computational cost, be sensitive to priors, and struggle with non-conjugate models. In high-dimensional, dynamic recommender systems, maintaining posteriors can be costly and fail real-time needs.
In practice, non-stationarity appears as reward drift, dynamic arm sets, changing context, and time-varying popularity. Theoretically, the regret becomes O\left(\sqrt{\left(KTlogT\right)}+V_T\right), where V_T is the total variation. When V_T=\mathrm{\Omega}\left(T\right), linear regret is unavoidable  [4]. Thus traditional methods degrade sharply in highly dynamic settings.
Given the specifics of recommendation, drawbacks are amplified. First, the set of arms is changing addition of new arms leads to the problem of missing statistics and indices under fixed assumptions. Secondly, items have temporal patterns new items can be highly rated at first and then become unpopular; some items are popular classics but in a seasonal manner; some genres can be preferred in particular periods of time. Without the time decay and popularity modeling the quality of recommendation suffers. Thirdly, new items and new users do not have history and without transferring information bases on similarities exploration goes slowly and user experience degrades.
Researchers proposed improvements such as sliding windows, exponential weighting, and change-point detection. However, window size sensitivity, data discard, aggressive forgetting, limited theory, detection delays, false alarms, and threshold tuning all remain [5][6]. These methods do not systematically handle time information, dynamic arm sets, and cold starts, motivating new designs that meet these needs simultaneously.

Strategies Geared toward Real-World Application Differentiation
The proposed algorithms tackle fundamental challenges of non-stationary recommendation through complementary means. To satisfy memory, latency, and complexity constraints, we employ a suite of engineering optimisations. Firstly, we utilise efficient data structures to speed up execution-hash maps for constant-time lookups and updates, and priority queues to maintain UCB orderings. Secondly, we implement incremental updates to avoid re-computation of previously acquired results. Thirdly, we exploit parallel computation to distribute per-arm UCB computations across processors. For large-scale systems we also apply intelligent memory management-dynamic allocation, garbage-collection tuning, and memory-pool techniques, which reduces overall footprint an improves access efficiency.
Compared to previous improvements, our methods are fine-tuned and optimized. Sliding‑window approaches can deal with non‑stationarity but are very sensitive to the window size and they simply throw away out‑of‑window information; our popularity‑aware component uses adaptive time windows and weight adjustment to better balance historical evidence and recent trend. Exponential‑weighting methods emphasize recency but the forgetting factor may be too aggressive and is often not theoretically justified; our algorithms are theoretically motivated and empirically validated to offer more convincing performance guarantees. Change‑point detection may suffer from detection delay, false alarms, and difficult threshold selection. By observing arm‑set changes in real time, dynamic arm management reacts promptly without depending on sophisticated thresholds, hence enhancing practicality in deployment.
In e-commerce, dynamic management reacts quickly to item rotation; popularity awareness catches seasonality; cold-start techniques improve coverage for new items and users. In content recommendation (e.g., news), dynamic updates deal with fast content rotation, popularity follows hot topics, and cold-start transfer personalizes for new users and items, proving useful for deployment [11] . The modular architecture allows for independent components and adaptation between environments through dynamic parameter adjustment, adaptive learning rate and intelligent resource allocation.
Research Shortcomings and Future Prospects
Although the proposed algorithms exhibit significant improvements under non‑stationarity, there are several issues that need further investigation. First, from the perspective of algorithm design, the present approaches depend largely on heuristics and empirical tuning, without rigorous theoretical analysis and formal proofs. Although experimental results suggest strong practical performance, the lack of guarantees poses uncertainty in terms of stability and robustness especially in extreme cases when the environment changes drastically or the data distribution shifts radically, where performance could deteriorate rapidly. This hinders deployment in safety-critical settings.
Second, parameter tuning still needs a manual setting of some crucial hyperparameters, like the popularity weight coefficient (\alpha), the knowledge‑transfer coefficient (\beta), and the window size\ (w). The performance is sensitive to them and the best values may significantly differ between application contexts. Thus, without automatic tuning, the barrier of entry is kept high and misuse might lead to performance deterioration, which is particularly problematic in industrial settings with strong robustness requirements.
Third, the computational complexity brings challenges at scale. In particular, the similarity‑matrix computation in the Cold‑Start UCB has time complexity O\left((K^2)\right). When the item pool is very large, the cost becomes unbearable even with optimizations. Memory usage also increases quickly with the scale of data, which raises the cost for deployment and impacts system stability. The experimental evaluation also has its limitations: it is mainly based on MovieLens 25M, a popular academic dataset whose rating signals differ from the rich multi‑signal feedback (clicks, purchases, shares, etc.) in production, which limits the external validity of the conclusions. The evaluation also focuses on the cumulative reward and regret, without evaluations on latency, diversity and user satisfaction, which does not compltetly reflect the user experience in real systems.
Generality and extensibility of the algorithms also have limitations. Current enhancements are specialized for recommendation‑specific concerns dynamic arm management for pool changes, popularity awareness for temporal information, and cold‑start transfer based on item similarity and their usefulness in other bandit domains (e.g., online advertising, resource allocation) remains unverified. The investigation centers around a single recommendation task, excluding multi‑task and multi‑objective optimization, such as jointly optimizing of CTR, conversion and satisfaction. Algorithms also do not yet enable multiple‑arm selection or list‑wise recommendation mechanisms that better align with practical needs, limiting application prospects in production systems.
Prospective works include: 1.Theoretical analysis: Regret bounds and convergence guarantees under non-stationarity; precise models for environmental changes with stronger stability guarantees. 2. Hyperparameter adaptation: Adaptive mechanisms for selecting hyperparameters (e.g., meta-learning, Bayesian optimization, bandit-based configuration); coupling effects and sensitivity between parameters [12][13]; 3.Computational efficiency and scalability: Approximation, parallelization, incremental updates; distributed and streaming frameworks for large-scale real-time recommendation; 4.Extension to multi-objective optimization and complex recommendation scenarios: Pareto optimization, list-wise and multiple-arm selection bandits; effectiveness validation on various tasks for enhanced practicality and deployability.
Conclusion
This paper studies the challenges of recommendation systems in non-stationary environments and presents three enhanced multi-armed bandit algorithms. Firstly, a dynamic arm management UCB algorithm is proposed, which utilizes a flexible arm set management mechanism to address the limitation of traditional algorithms in dealing with dynamic candidate pools. It adjusts to the inclusion of new items and the exclusion of old items, thus avoiding the index overflow problem in traditional algorithms. Secondly, a time-window based UCB algorithm is designed, which introduces popularity into the UCB calculation by weighting recent popularity information via temporal decay. This helps the algorithm better cope with dynamic changes in user preferences. With popularity integrated into the UCB calculation, a better trade-off between exploration and exploitation is obtained. This design helps the algorithm retain historical information while rapidly adapting to current popularity trends, delivering more accurate recommendations that match current preferences to users. Lastly, a cold-start UCB algorithm is proposed, which alleviates the cold-start issue in new item recommendations via similarity-based knowledge transfer. It increases sensitivity to new items by utilizing historical information of similar items and dynamically changing exploration weights to compensate for the exploration-exploitation imbalance. This design greatly enhances the accuracy of new item recommendations, providing users with a more personalized recommendation experience.
From the theoretical aspect, this paper extends the application scenarios of the multi-armed bandit algorithms and offers new ideas for designing algorithms in non-stationary settings. The proposed algorithms retain the theoretical merits of classical bandit models and introduce novel mechanisms to overcome the critical issues encountered by recommendation systems. These achievements bring new perspectives to the theoretical study of multi-armed bandits and offer useful references to related studies. From the practical aspect, this paper offers effective approaches to optimize recommendation systems in dynamic environments by exploiting temporal information and similarity-based knowledge transferring. The enhanced algorithms greatly improve the recommendation accuracy and user experience, and provide strong technical support for real world recommendation applications.
In conclusion, this paper contributes algorithmic designs and optimisations targeting non-stationary settings, overcoming key issues of recommendation in such environments. The outcomes advance both the theoretical study of multi-armed bandits and offer practical remedies to recommendation applications. They pave the way for future studies and suggest new research avenues in related areas.
